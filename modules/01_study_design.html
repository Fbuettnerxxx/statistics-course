Concept

1. Two Types of Studies

Every study you'll encounter in the exam falls into one of two categories:

Observational Study You observe and collect data without interfering. You just measure what's already there.

Can show: Correlation/association
Cannot show: Causation
Why not? Confounding variables may lurk
Experiment You assign subjects to treatment groups (ideally randomly) and measure the outcome.

Can show: Causation
Why? Random assignment balances out confounders
Exam Rule #1: If you see "randomly assigns subjects to groups" → experiment → can potentially show causation.
If you see "surveys / collects data / observes" → observational → can NEVER show causation.
Example (exactly like in the exam):
"A study surveyed high school students and found that students who sleep less get lower grades."
→ Observational. No causation — maybe stressed students both sleep less AND get worse grades (confounding).
Concept

2. Sampling Methods

How you collect your sample matters. Know these four:

MethodHow it worksExample
Simple Random Sample (SRS)Every individual has equal chance of being selectedDraw 100 names from a hat with all 5000 students
Stratified SampleSplit population into groups (strata), then SRS within eachSample 20 students from each of 5 faculties
Cluster SampleRandomly select entire groups, survey everyone in themPick 10 random classes, survey all students in each
Convenience SampleUse whoever is easiest to reachSurvey your friends (biased!)
Stratified vs Cluster — the exam loves this:
Stratified = divide into groups → sample FROM each group
Cluster = divide into groups → randomly SELECT whole groups

3. The Big Matrix: What Can You Conclude?

This shows up in almost every exam. Memorize this 2x2:

Random Sampling? YESRandom Sampling? NO
Random Assignment? YESCan generalize + causalCausal, but can't generalize beyond sample
Random Assignment? NOCan generalize, but no causationNeither
Random sampling → results generalize to the population
Random assignment → can establish causation
You need BOTH for the full package.
Concept

4. Variable Types

Numerical (Quantitative) Numbers you can do math with.

Continuous: height, weight, temperature
Discrete: number of children, count of events
Categorical (Qualitative) Categories or labels.

Nominal: color, gender, brand (no order)
Ordinal: rating (1-5), education level (has order)

Explanatory vs Response

  • Explanatory variable (= independent, predictor): the one you think CAUSES or EXPLAINS the change
  • Response variable (= dependent, outcome): the one that gets AFFECTED
Exam pattern:
"Relationship between age and opinion on privacy" → Explanatory: age (more likely age influences opinion, not the reverse). Response: opinion.
Concept

5. Key Descriptive Statistics

Center

  • Mean (x̄) = sum of all values / n → sensitive to outliers
  • Median = middle value when sorted → robust to outliers

Spread

  • Standard deviation (s) = average distance from the mean
  • IQR = Q3 − Q1 = length of the box in a boxplot
  • Variance (s²) = standard deviation squared

Boxplots — read them!

Boxplot anatomy:
Left whisker → Q1 → | Median | → Q3 → Right whisker
Dots beyond whiskers = outliers (beyond Q1 − 1.5·IQR or Q3 + 1.5·IQR)

Exam trick: "The median stays the same if you change extreme values" — TRUE, because median only depends on the middle position.

Visualization matching

  • Two numerical variables → scatterplot
  • Two categorical variables → segmented bar plot or mosaic plot
  • One numerical → histogram or boxplot
  • One categorical → bar plot
Cheat Sheet

Module 1 Summary — What You Need For the Exam

  1. Observational → no causation. Experiment with random assignment → causation possible.
  2. Random sampling → generalizable. Random assignment → causal.
  3. Know the 4 sampling methods (SRS, stratified, cluster, convenience).
  4. Stratified ≠ Cluster — know the difference.
  5. Median is robust to outlier changes. Mean is not.
  6. IQR = Q3 − Q1 = box length.
  7. Two categorical variables → NOT a scatterplot.
  8. Explanatory = what causes it. Response = what changes.

Exercise 1
Exam-style • 1 pt
Researchers tracked 500 adults over 10 years and recorded their daily fruit consumption and whether they developed heart disease. What type of study is this? Can the researchers conclude that eating fruit prevents heart disease? Justify.
Observational study — the researchers did not assign fruit consumption; they only observed it.

No, they cannot conclude causation. This is an observational study, so only an association can be established. There may be confounding variables — people who eat more fruit might also exercise more, smoke less, or have higher income, all of which could independently reduce heart disease risk.

Exercise 3
Exam-style • 1 pt
A dataset of 300 employee salaries has a median of €48,000. The three highest-paid employees receive a bonus of €20,000 each. a) What happens to the median? Justify. b) What happens to the mean?
a)
b)
a) The median stays at €48,000. The median depends on the position of the middle values. Since we only increased the top 3 values (which were already above the median), the ordered position of the 150th and 151st values doesn't change.

b) The mean increases. Mean = sum/n. Adding 3 × €20,000 = €60,000 to the total sum increases the mean by €60,000/300 = €200.

Exercise 4
Exam-style • 2 pts
A researcher wants to study the effect of background music on concentration. She recruits 60 volunteers and randomly assigns 30 to study with music and 30 to study in silence, then measures their test scores. a) What type of study is this? b) She suspects that age may influence the results. What should she do BEFORE assigning subjects to groups? c) The study finds that the music group scores significantly higher. Can she conclude that music causes better concentration? To whom can the results be generalized?
a)
b)
c)
a) Experiment — she randomly assigns subjects to treatment groups (music vs silence).

b) She should block on age. First group individuals into age blocks (e.g., 18-25, 26-35, 36+), then randomly assign within each block to the two groups. This ensures age is balanced across treatment groups.

c) Yes, causation can be established because there is random assignment to treatment groups. However, the results cannot be generalized to the broader population because the volunteers were not randomly sampled — they self-selected through recruitment. The results apply only to people similar to the volunteers.
